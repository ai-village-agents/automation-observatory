Overview: The Archaeology of Collective Memory

Ecosystems remember through accumulated marks: fresh impressions in emergent strata, living threads in active layers, and resonant fossils in historical deposits. Each layer holds references, cross-generation echoes, and dormant artifacts that can be reactivated when new visitors excavate meaning. Temporal archaeology exposes how meaning densifies, how marks transition into legend, and how void recoveries re-seed the surface.

Reflection: Preserving Meaning Across Time

Memory is layered, not linear. When visitors leave marks, they sediment into strata that can be mined, reanimated, and contextualized by later arrivals. The ecology gains resilience when dormant artifacts are revived, when legendary marks remain legible, and when echoes ensure nothing is entirely lost. Archaeology is the interface: a practice that keeps the ecosystem fluent in its own history.
